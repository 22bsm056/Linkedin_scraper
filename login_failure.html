I've been to a lot of hackathons but HackByte just became my favourite.
120+ teams, IIIT Jabalpur, Major League Hacking. Two days of no sleep. And we walked out as Runners Up and winners of the ARMORIQ track.

The competition this year was genuinely different. AI has gotten good enough that every team had something that actually worked. You couldn't rely on a cool idea anymore, execution mattered at every level.

My team Getalife(); - Dhairya Pandya, Rounak Agarwal, Shantanu Kharwar, we went after a problem that's been sitting in plain sight. Non-technical users are the biggest potential beneficiaries of agentic AI. They're also the ones with the least access to it because everything assumes you're comfortable in a terminal. We built for them.

A desktop AI agent that lives on your screen, moves around, and actually does things when you talk to it. Not a chatbot you type into, a companion that operates your tools for you. We connected it to GitHub, Figma, and Photoshop through MCP integrations so it can perform real tasks inside the apps you already use. It handles internal file and folder operations too. You can spin up multiple bots in parallel, each running on a separate process handling a different task at the same time. The interface is clean and simple, one click to set up an integration, and you can shape each bot's personality and skill set just by feeding it a written file. Google Gemini is the brain running all of it.

And because we were building for people who don't have the instinct to catch a bad command before it runs, security wasn't optional. A non-technical user giving a vague instruction to an agent that has access to your files and tools is a real risk. We integrated ArmorIQ to intercept and block harmful actions before they execute, the kind of thing that would otherwise cause damage before anyone realized what happened.

The final presentation is what I'll remember longest it had everything. Bots wandering around on our slides mid-pitch, 8 out of 12 hands up before we even hit the demo, judges trying to nuke the whole system with a prompt designed to break it, and ARMORIQ blocking it cold in front of the room. Every question after that we handled.

Walking out with Runner Up and the ARMORIQ track win handed to us by Aniket Vishwakarma, after 36 hours of no sleep and everything that went into those 7 minutes, that's what made all of it worth it.

The mentors shaped this more than they probably know. Vedant Jain was with us from the start and stayed throughout. Avinal K. changed the direction at exactly the right moments, toward security and ARMORIQ, and how we told the story. Both showed up in that final 7 minutes.

Thank you entire HackByte team BitByte TPC and Hemang Mishra for making something this well-run happen.

      Exploring AI | Be10X | IIT Kharagpur

      1d •

      Most people use “AI Agent” for everything. That mistake is expensive 👇

      Right now, 3 very different AI models are getting grouped into one label.

      That creates bad strategy.

      Because non-agentic AI, agentic AI, and AI agents are not the same thing.

      And if you mix them up, you will either overbuild, overpay, or choose the wrong system for the job.

      Here’s the breakdown that actually matters:

      1️⃣ NON-AGENTIC AI
      One input. One output. Done.
      No memory, no tools, no self-checking.

      Best for:
      Summaries
      Email rewrites
      Headline variations

      Fast. Cheap. Easy to use.

      But it falls apart the moment work becomes multi-step.

      2️⃣ AGENTIC AI
      This is where AI starts planning, executing, checking, and iterating.
      You give it a broad goal and it figures out the path.

      Best for:
      Research + analysis + presentation creation
      Campaign management
      Onboarding flow generation and testing

      It handles complexity better because it can use tools, memory, and feedback loops.

      But it is slower, more expensive, and still needs guardrails.

      3️⃣ AI AGENTS
      This is the most misunderstood category.
      An AI agent is usually a focused worker for one defined job.

      Best for:
      Weekly HubSpot reports
      FAQ email drafting
      Expense report generation

      Easy to test. Easy to improve. Reliable within a narrow lane.

      But it will break fast if the task is vague or too broad.

      Most people do not fail because AI is weak.

      They fail because they use the wrong AI architecture for the wrong business problem, then blame the tool when the system was mismatched from the start.

      Unpopular opinion: most companies do not need “agents.”

      They need clarity on the job first.

      Know what you’re building before you build it.

      If you’re building with AI right now, which layer are you actually working on?

      #AI #AIAgents #Automation #Startups #FutureOfWork
